Wei book \begin{eqnarray*} \phi_{kk} = \frac{ \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_1 & \rho_2 & \cdots & \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{1} \\ \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \cdots & \rho_{{k}-3} & \rho_{2} \\ \vdots & & & \cdots & & \vdots \\ \vdots & & & \cdots & & \vdots \\ \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{{k}-3} & \rho_{{k}-4} & \cdots & 1 & \rho_{k-1} \\ \rho_{{k}-1} & \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{{k}-3} & \cdots & \rho_1 & \rho_k \\ \end{pmatrix} } { \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_1 & \rho_2 & \cdots & \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{{k}-1} \\ \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \cdots & \rho_{{k}-3} & \rho_{{k}-2} \\ \vdots & & & \cdots & & \vdots \\ \vdots & & & \cdots & & \vdots \\ \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{{k}-3} & \rho_{{k}-4} & \cdots & 1 & \rho_1 \\ \rho_{{k}-1} & \rho_{{k}-2} & \rho_{{k}-3} & \cdots & \rho_1 & 1 \\ \end{pmatrix} \, . } \end{eqnarray*} We know that $\phi_{00}=1$ and $\phi_{11}=\rho_1$ . For $k>1$ , For an MA(1) process we found that $\rho_i=0$ for $i\ge 2$ . Then we should evaluate \begin{eqnarray*} \phi_{kk} = \frac{ \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_1 & 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & 0 & \rho_{1} \\ \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \ddots & & & \vdots & 0 \\ 0 & \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \ddots & & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 & \vdots \\ \vdots & & & \ddots & \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \vdots \\ \vdots & & & & \ddots & \rho_1 & 1 & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & 0 & \rho_1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} } { \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_1 & 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & 0 \\ \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \ddots & & & & \vdots \\ 0 & \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & \ddots & & & \vdots \\ \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & & \vdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots & 0 \\ \vdots & & & \ddots & \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 & 0 \\ \vdots & & & & \ddots & \rho_1 & 1 & \rho_1 \\ 0 & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & \cdots & 0 & \rho_1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} } \end{eqnarray*} where each matrix has $k$ rows and columns. We need to evaluate these determinants. For the numerator, we expand the determinant using the last column. Then After eliminating the first row and last column we end up with a upper triangular matrix with $\rho_1$ in the diagonal. Then the numerator has the value of $\rho_1^k$ . For the denominator we solve a more general problem. \begin{eqnarray*} A_k = \det \begin{pmatrix} a & b & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ c & a & b & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & c & a & b & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \cdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & & \vdots & c & a \end{pmatrix} . \end{eqnarray*} where $n$ is the number of rows and columns We expand by the first row and find \begin{eqnarray} A_k = a A_{k-1} - b ( c A_{k-2}) \label{solsAk} \end{eqnarray} This is a homogeneous difference equation with initial conditions: $A_1=a$ , $A_2=a^2 - bc$ . The characteristic polynomial is \begin{eqnarray*} P(\lambda) = 1 - a \lambda + b c \lambda^2 , \end{eqnarray*} with roots \begin{eqnarray*} r_{1,2} = \frac{a \pm \sqrt{a^2 - 4 bc}}{2bc} \end{eqnarray*} We follow the method to solve linear difference equations Since $a=1, b=c=\rho_1$ we have that \begin{eqnarray*} r_{1,2} = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - \rho_1^2}}{2 \rho_1^2} . \end{eqnarray*} Then, the roots are different and real (since $0 ), so the solution is given by \begin{eqnarray*} A_k = C_1 r_1^k + C_2 r_2^k \quad , \quad (1) \end{eqnarray*} with $C_1, C_2$ to be found using the initial conditions. Let us reduce the initial conditions to simple expressions. If we assume $A_0=1$ , then $A_1=a$ and $A_2=a A_1 - bc A_0 = a^2 -bc$ which matches the second order solution for $k=2$ . Then we use the initial conditions as \begin{eqnarray*} A_0=1 \quad , \quad A_1 = a. \end{eqnarray*} The solution, for in terms of $\rho_1$ is not obvious except for the first coefficients For example, clearly $A_1 = a =1$ , $A_2=a^2-bc = 1- \rho_1^2$ and $A_3 = a A_2 - bc A_1 = 1-\rho_1^2 - \rho^2 = 1 - 2 \rho_1^2$ . Now ,from the equation for $\rho_1$ in an MA process, $\rho_1=\beta_1/(1 + \beta_1^2)$ and \begin{eqnarray*} \phi_{11} = \rho_1 = \frac{\beta_1}{1 + \beta_1^2} \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \phi_{22} &=& \frac{\rho_1^2}{1 - \rho_1^2} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^2}{(1 + \beta_1^2)^2 \left (1 - \frac{\beta_1^2}{1 + \beta_1^2} \right )} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^2}{(1 + \beta_1^2)^2 - \beta_1^2} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^2}{1 + 2 \beta_1^2 + \beta_1^4 - \beta_1^2} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^2}{1 + \beta_1^2 + \beta_1^4} \end{eqnarray*} \begin{eqnarray*} \phi_{33} &=& \frac{\rho_1^3}{1 - 2 \rho_1^2} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^3}{(1 + \beta_1^2)^3 \left ( 1 - 2 \frac{\beta_1^2}{( 1 + \beta_1^2)^2} \right )} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^3}{(1 + \beta_1^2)^3 - 2 \beta_1^2 (1 + \beta_1^2)} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^3}{1 + 3 \beta_1^2 + 3 \beta_1^4 + \beta_1^6 - 2 \beta_1^2 - 2 \beta_1^4} \\ &=& \frac{\beta_1^3}{1 + \beta_1^2 + \beta_1^4 + \beta_1^6 } \end{eqnarray*} There is a clear pattern that indicates \begin{eqnarray} \phi_{kk} = \frac{\beta_1^k}{\sum_{i=0}^k \beta_1^{2i}}. \label{pktoappd} \end{eqnarray} This pattern can be easily shown by induction and the recursion (1) above
