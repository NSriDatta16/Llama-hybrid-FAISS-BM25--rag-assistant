An approach that almost works is as follows: Note that $\left(\vec{b}^{\top}\vec{b} - \vec{b}^{\top}\vec{a}\right) / \sqrt{\vec{b}^{\top}\vec{b}}$ 'looks like' $\vec{z}^{\top} \vec{c}$, where $\vec{c}$ is a unit-length vector (it is actually $\vec{b}$ scaled to unit length), and $\vec{z} = \vec{b} - \vec{a} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0,I\right)$. If it were the case that $\vec{c}$ were independent of $\vec{z}$, then one could claim that $\vec{b}^{\top}\vec{b} + Z_{\alpha} \sqrt{\vec{b}^{\top}\vec{b}}$ was a $\alpha$ confidence bound, where $Z_{\alpha}$ is the $\alpha$ quantile of the normal. However, $\vec{c}$ is not independent of $\vec{z}$. It tends to be 'aligned with' $\vec{z}$. Now, when $\vec{a}^{\top}\vec{a} \gg 1$, $\vec{c}$ is essentially independent, and the confidence bound above gives proper coverage.
