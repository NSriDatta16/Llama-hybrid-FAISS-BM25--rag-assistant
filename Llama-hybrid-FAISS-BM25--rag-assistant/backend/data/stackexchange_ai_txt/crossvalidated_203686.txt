So let call hypothesis $H_1$ is $\{(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) \mbox{ such that } \theta_1 Then supposing that I have a model to infer $P(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3 | x)$ under the complete parameter space $H_1 \cup H_2 $ then $P(H_1|x)$ can simply be computed as: $$ P(H_1|x)=\int_m^M P(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3|x) 1_{\theta_1>\theta_2>\theta_3}(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) d\theta_1 d\theta_2 d\theta_3 $$ by simply counting the posterior MCMC samples satisfying the condition and $P(H_2|x)=1-P(H_1|x)$. So it remains to extract the associated $\frac{P(H_2)}{P(H_1)}$ by simply considering the subpart of $H_1$ over the overall parameter space: $$ P(H_1)=\int p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) \cdot 1_{\theta_1>\theta_2>\theta_3}(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3) d\theta_1 d\theta_2 d\theta_3 $$ which can be computed analytically for the prior $p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3)$ associated to my posterior and again $ P(H_2)=1-p(H_1)$. So the two options are very close each other.In particular if $P(H_1|x)>0.95$ then the BF would be greater than (0.95/0.05)*((5/6)/(1/6))=95
