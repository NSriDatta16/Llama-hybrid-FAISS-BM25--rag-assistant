To complete Tim's answer, from the useful references given there: Suppose the quantities $\{X_i\} := \{X_1, X_2, \dotsc\}$ and $\{Y_j\}$ can have values $\{x\}$ and $\{y\}$ from two discrete sets. The assumption of infinite partial exchangeability for their joint probabilities means $$ \mathrm{P}(X_1=x_1, \dotsc, X_n=x_n,\; Y_1=y_1, \dotsc, Y_m=y_m) ={}\\ \mathrm{P}(X_1=x_{\pi(1)}, \dotsc, X_n=x_{\pi(n)},\; Y_1=y_{\sigma(1)}, \dotsc, Y_m=y_{\sigma(m)}),\\ \text{for all $m,n$, and all permutations $\pi,\sigma$ of $\{1,\dotsc,n\}$ and $\{1,\dotsc,m\}$.} $$ Then the probability above can be expressed in this integral form: $$ \mathrm{P}(X_1=x_1, \dotsc, X_n=x_n,\; Y_1=y_1, \dotsc, Y_m=y_m) ={}\\ \iint \prod_{i=1}^n p_{x_i}\; \prod_{j=1}^m q_{y_j}\; f(\mathbf{p},\mathbf{q})\;\mathrm{d}\mathbf{p}\,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{q}, $$ where $\mathbf{p} := \{p_x\}$ and $\mathbf{q} := \{q_y\}$ are distributions over the possible values $\{x\}$ and $\{y\}$, and $f$ is a probability density over all such pairs of distributions. Another useful reference for partial exchangeability, with examples, is Bernardo & Smith's Bayesian Theory (Wiley 2000), especially ยง 4.6.
