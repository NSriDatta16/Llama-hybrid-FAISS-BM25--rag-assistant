Bayesian posterior density estimation, given data and a prior: Can the denominator be evaluated in two different ways?

Assume the simple, well-known scenario: Data = $(x_j, y_j)_{j=1}^{n}$ , and that, as usual, the $n$ data points are drawn iid. The $x$ 's may be considered non-random, but the $y$ 's are observations from a random $Y$ . \begin{align*} \text{Posterior:} \quad f_{\Theta|\text{Data}}(\theta | \text{dat}) &= f_{\Theta | Y_1,\cdots,Y_n} (\theta | y_1,\cdots,y_n; x_1,\cdots,x_n) \\ &= \frac{f_{Y_1,\cdots,Y_n | \Theta} (y_1,\cdots,y_n | \theta; x_1,\cdots,x_n) f_{\Theta}(\theta)}{f_{Y_1,\cdots,Y_n} (y_1,\cdots,y_n; x_1,\cdots,x_n)} \\ &= \frac{f_{Y_1,\cdots,Y_n | \Theta} (y_1,\cdots,y_n | \theta; x_1,\cdots,x_n) f_{\Theta}(\theta)}{\int_{\theta} f_{Y_1,\cdots,Y_n | \Theta} (y_1,\cdots,y_n | \theta; x_1,\cdots,x_n) f_{\Theta}(\theta) d\theta} \\ &= \frac{f_{\Theta}(\theta) \prod_{j=1}^{n} f_{Y|\Theta}(y_j | \theta ; x_j)}{\int_{\theta} f_{\Theta}(\theta) \prod_{j=1}^{n} f_{Y|\Theta}(y_j | \theta ; x_j) d\theta} \end{align*} The above is perfectly understood by me. But I am confused about one question: Why can't the denominator of the posterior density be expressed as (since data are iid) $\prod_{j=1}^{n} f_{Y}(y_j; x_j) = \prod_{j=1}^{n} \int_{\theta} f_{Y}(y_j, \theta; x_j) d\theta = \prod_{j=1}^{n} \int_{\theta} f_{Y}(y_j| \theta; x_j) f_{\Theta}(\theta) d\theta$ , which, being a product of integrals, seems to be different from the integral of a product in the standard formula. What am I missing here? Thank you.
