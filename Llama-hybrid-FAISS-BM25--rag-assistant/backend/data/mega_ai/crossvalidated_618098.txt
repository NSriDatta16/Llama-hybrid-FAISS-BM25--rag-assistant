$A^{-1}$ in the Triangular factorisation of a MA(1) process

I am trying to confirm the what the $A^{-1}$ actually is in a triangular factorisation of the variance-covariance of a MA(1) process. And what I have found doesn't seem to match what is on Hamilton p.129 (Time Series Analysis (Hamilton, 1994)). Does anyone know which is correct? On Hamilton p.129, the variance-covariance matrix of a MA(1) process can be decomposed using the triangular factorisation thusly: $\Omega = ADA'$ $ \hspace{2mm}\text{where $A$ is given by}$ $$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{\theta}{1+{\theta}^2} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2)}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4} & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ ... & ... & ... & ... & ... \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-2)})}{1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-1)}} & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ therefore we can construct an auxiliary time series thusly $\widetilde{y} = A^{-1}(y-\mu) $ $$\text{where}\hspace{5mm} \widetilde{y_t} = y_t - \mu - [\frac{{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-2)})}{1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-1)}}]\widetilde{y}_{t-1} $$ this implies the $A^{-1}$ being: $$ A^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{-\theta}{1+{\theta}^2} & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{-{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2)}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4} & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ ... & ... & ... & ... & ... \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{-{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-2)})}{1+{\theta}^2 +{\theta}^4+...+{\theta}^{2(t-1)}} & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ However, when I tried to inverse the same A matrix using the SymbolLab calculator I got this $$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{-\theta}{1+{\theta}^2} & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{{\theta}^2}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4} & \frac{-{\theta}(1+{\theta}^2)}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4} & 1 & 0 \\ \frac{-\theta^3}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4+{\theta}^6} & \frac{\theta^2}{\theta^4+1} & \frac{-{\theta}({\theta}^4+{\theta}^2+1)}{1+{\theta}^2+{\theta}^4+{\theta}^6} & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ Does anyone know which is actually correct?
