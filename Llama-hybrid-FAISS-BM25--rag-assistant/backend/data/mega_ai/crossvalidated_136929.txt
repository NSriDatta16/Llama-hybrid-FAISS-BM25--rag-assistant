With having a technical border TB for $a=0$ a simplified approach by H. Schneider is very usefull to calculate the mean $\mu_t$ and standard deviation $\sigma_t$ of the truncated normal distribution: calculate the mean $\mu$ and the standard deviation $\sigma$ (entire population!) for the data set: $\mu = \bar{x}= \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i$ $\sigma = s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}$ check if the technical border $TB=a=0$ has a valid distance to the average $\bar{x}$: consideration of $TB = a$ is not necessary when $\bar{x} \le 3s$ calculate $\omega, P_3(\omega), P_4(\omega)$ and $Q(\omega)$: $\omega = \frac{s^2}{(a-\bar{x})^2}$ $P_3(\omega) = 1+5,74050101\omega - 13,53427037\omega^2 + 6,88665552\omega^3$ $P_4(\omega) = -0,00374615 + 0,17462558\omega - 2,87168509\omega^2 + 17,48932655\omega^3 - 11,91716546\omega^4$ $Q(\omega) = \frac{P_4(\omega)}{P_3(\omega)}$ check if $\omega \le 0,57081$, otherwise the mean $\mu_t$ is $ calculate $\mu_t$ and $\sigma_t$ for the truncated normal distribution: $\mu_t = \bar{x} + Q(\omega) \cdot (a-\bar{x})$ $\sigma_{t}^{2} = s^2 + Q(\omega) \cdot (a-\bar{x})^2$ That's all...
