These kind of problems often become more intuitive when you make a more extreme sample: Let's compute the average wealth of people in a village by considering the group of millionairs and the group of others Wealth N people average person wealth millionairs 4 000 000 2 2 000 000 others 19 960 000 998 20 000 tot 23 960 000 1 000 method 1 average of the group averages ... the average of 2 000 000 and 20 000 $$\frac{2\, 020\, 000}{2} = \frac{2 \, 000 \, 000 + 20 \, 000}{1+1} = 1\, 010 \, 000$$ method 2 average of the individuals ... the average of 2 000 000 and a lot of 20 000's. $$\begin{array}{} \frac{23\, 960\, 000}{1 \, 000} &=&\frac{2 \times 2 \, 000 \, 000 + 998 \times 20 \, 000}{2+998} &&\\ &=& \frac{2 \, 000 \, 000 + 2 \, 000 \, 000 + 20 \, 000 + 20 \, 000 + ... + 20 \, 000+ 20 \, 000}{1+1+...+1+1} &=& 23 \, 960 \end{array}$$ You could see the difference as is in how often you count the 2 000 000 and 20 000. a general formula could be: $$ \frac{\text{total } X}{ \text{total } N} = \frac{N_1 \frac{X_1}{N_1} + N_2 \frac{X_2}{N_2} + N_3 \frac{X_3}{N_3} + ... }{N_1 + N_2 + N_3 + ...}$$ but not $$ \frac{\text{total } X}{ \text{total } N} = \frac{1 \frac{X_1}{N_1} + 1 \frac{X_2}{N_2} + 1 \frac{X_3}{N_3} + ... }{1+1+1 + ...}$$
