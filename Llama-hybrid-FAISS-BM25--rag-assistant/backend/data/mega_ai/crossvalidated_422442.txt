I have used the following link as a reference: https://web.stanford.edu/~hastie/glmnet/glmnet_alpha.html#log When printing the model as can be seen even in the above link I am getting a 3 column matrix where the first column represents the number of non-zero coef, the 2nd column the %deviance and 3rd the value of lambda. ## ## Call: glmnet(x = x, y = y, weights = c(rep(1, 50), rep(2, 50)), alpha = 0.2, nlambda = 20) ## ## Df %Dev Lambda ## [1,] 0 0.000 7.94000 ## [2,] 4 0.179 4.89000 ## [3,] 7 0.444 3.01000 ## [4,] 7 0.657 1.85000 ## [5,] 8 0.785 1.14000 ## [6,] 9 0.854 0.70300 ## [7,] 10 0.887 0.43300 ## [8,] 11 0.902 0.26700 ## [9,] 14 0.910 0.16400 ## [10,] 17 0.914 0.10100 ## [11,] 17 0.915 0.06230 ## [12,] 17 0.916 0.03840 ## [13,] 19 0.916 0.02360 ## [14,] 20 0.916 0.01460 ## [15,] 20 0.916 0.00896 ## [16,] 20 0.916 0.00552 ## [17,] 20 0.916 0.00340 A large valye of lambda indicates relatively relaxed constraint as this allows more variables in the model. In the above example we have for example lambda = 0.00340 with 20 non-zero coef while lambda = 7.94 with 0 non zero coef. How is this possible?
